1) How does the happiness of a respondent relate to the marriage status?

sex <- factor(dat$`Respondents sex`)
happy <- factor(dat$`General happiness`)
happy <- factor(happy, levels(happy)[c(6:1)])
marital <- factor(dat$`Marital status`)
marital <- factor(marital, levels(marital)[c(2,3,6,1,5,4)])
df <- data.frame(happy, marital, sex)
df <- df[marital != 'No answer' & (happy == 'Not too happy' | happy == 'Pretty happy' | happy == 'Very happy'), ]
df <- na.omit(df)
ggplot(df, aes(fill=happy, y=1,x=marital)) + geom_bar(position="fill", stat="identity") + labs(y='Percentage (%)', x='Marital Status', title='Marital Status vs General Happiness', fill='General Happiness')

There is evidence to support a relationship between marital status and general happiness. Married respondents had the greatest proportion of Very happy responses and the lowest proportion of Not too happy responses, indicating married respondents have the greatest general happiness. Separated respondents had the lowest proportion of Very happy responses and the greatest proportion of Not too happy responses, indicating that they have the least general happiness. Approximately 90% of married respondents reported they were either Very happy or Pretty happy, followed by 85% of never married respondents, 80% of widowed and divorced respondents, and 70% of separated respondents. Companionship could be a possible cause of the greater happiness of married respondents while separated respondents may be in the process of a divorce. Their unhappiness could be due to an ongoing divorce or fresh wounds from an ending relationship.

The variables Marital status (with No answer removed) and General happiness (with Dont know, No answer, and Not applicable removed) were used to answer this question.

7) Is there a relationship between age and general happiness of never married respondents?

a <- strtoi(dat$`Age of respondent`)
a1<- case_when(a < 35 ~ 'Young', between(a,35,52)~'Young-Mid', between(a, 53,70)~'Mid-Old', a >= 71 ~ 'Old')
a2 <- factor(a1)
a2 <- factor(a2, levels(a2)[c(3,4,1,2)])
h <- factor(dat$`General happiness`)
h <- factor(h, levels(h)[c(6:1)])
m <- factor(dat$`Marital status`)
df2 <- data.frame(a2, h, m)
df2 <- df2[m == 'Never married' & (h=='Very happy' | h=='Pretty happy' | h=='Not too happy'),]
df2 <- na.omit(df2)
ggplot(df2, aes(fill=h, y=1,x=a2)) + geom_bar(position="fill", stat="identity") + labs(y='Percentage (%)', x='Age Group', title='Age vs General Happiness of Never Married Respondents', fill='General Happiness')

The Young-Mid age range (35-52) has the lowest proportion of very happy responses and the greatest proportion of not to happy responses. The Old age range (71 and up) has the highest proportion of very happy responses and is roughly tied for the lowest proportion of not too happy responses. Though the differences in proportions of responses are seem small, this may indicate that for people whove never married, older people are the most happy and people in the Young-Mid age range are the least happy.

People in the Young-Mid age range could still have an intention to get married and may fear that theyre running out of time, which could contribute to their overall unhappiness. Conversely, older people dont have those worries, and if they have intentions to get married, its for companionship and not starting a family.

The variables Age of Respondent, General happiness, and Marital status were used to answer this question.
